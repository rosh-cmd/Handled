Graphic Designer for Property & Construction Businesses — What to Look For

Most graphic designers can make something look good. The harder problem — the one that costs real estate agencies and construction companies the most — is finding one who understands what your industry actually produces.

Property and construction are both high-stakes, document-heavy, deadline-driven industries. The design requirements in each are specific. A designer who's spent their career on brand identity work or retail campaigns will spend the first three months learning what you already know — at your expense.

Handled Studio works with real estate agencies, individual agents, construction companies, and civil contractors across Melbourne and Australia. Here's what to look for — and how the two sectors differ.

Real estate agencies: what you actually need from a designer

Real estate marketing is a volume problem. It's not about making one thing look excellent — it's about making 30 to 60 assets every month look consistently excellent, on short deadlines, without the brief changing every time.

That requires a production designer, not a project designer. A production designer learns your brand once and executes quickly. They build templates that make every subsequent job faster. They know your headshot placement, your copy tone, and which printer you use — without being told every time.

Typical monthly design output for a real estate agency

Output type Typical monthly volume Who usually produces it
Listing kits (brochure, flyer, window card) 8–20 per month Admin, PA, or VA
Social tiles (Instagram, Facebook) 15–30 per month Principal agent or Canva DIY
EDM headers / campaign tiles 2–4 per month Marketing coordinator
Sold / just listed announcements 8–16 per month Admin or agent
Agent profiles / bio updates As needed Admin or freelancer
Open home signage Weekly Print supplier or admin

If your agency is producing 40–60 assets per month — which most mid-size agencies are — a project-based designer can't sustain that pace. The turnaround times don't work and the briefing overhead compounds over time.

Questions to ask a designer before you hire for real estate:

  • Do you work on retainer or project-by-project? The answer tells you whether they're set up for ongoing production.
  • Can you show me examples of listing kits and agent profiles? Not logos — everyday production output.
  • What's your turnaround on a standard listing kit? "3–5 business days" doesn't work when you sign on Tuesday and open on Saturday.
  • How do you handle revisions? Get clarity before you start, not after the third round of changes.
  • Are you available on short notice? Real estate doesn't run to a production schedule.

Where real estate agencies go wrong: Using Canva without locked templates, going offshore for cost savings, briefing by example rather than brand standard, and cycling through freelancers who never learn the brand well enough to produce independently. Each of these problems has the same underlying cause: no one owns the brand output. That's where the inconsistency starts.

Construction companies: what you actually need from a designer

The design requirements for a construction company are different from most industries in ways that are easy to underestimate.

Document design is the core skill. Not brand identity or social media — the capability statement, the tender submission, the project case study sheet. These are multi-page structured documents that require clear hierarchy, consistent formatting, and the ability to present complex technical information readably. That's a specialist skill most generalist designers don't have.

Photography treatment matters more than most companies realise. Construction businesses accumulate significant project photography that rarely gets used well. The ability to crop, sequence, and present site photography in a document or LinkedIn post so it does the work justice is more valuable than the ability to create original illustrations.

Tender deadlines are real deadlines. A designer who needs five business days is not viable when a submission closes Friday and it's Tuesday afternoon. Speed and reliability under deadline pressure are non-negotiable.

Questions to ask a designer before you hire for construction:

  • Have you produced capability statements or tender documents before? This is the first filter. If the answer is no, keep looking.
  • Can you show me examples of multi-page document design? Not poster work or logos — reports, proposals, capability statements.
  • What's your turnaround on a standard document revision? Tender bids involve multiple content changes as they develop.
  • Do you work on retainer or project-by-project? For ongoing collateral maintenance, project-by-project doesn't work.

The mistake most construction companies make is treating design as occasional rather than ongoing. A capability statement produced 18 months ago and never updated lists staff who've left and projects that no longer represent the company's current capability. That document is now a liability, not an asset — and procurement managers notice.

Design setup options — how they compare for construction companies

Setup Capability statement Tender submissions Ongoing marketing Cost structure
No designer Low — internal Word production Inconsistent None Low visible cost, high hidden cost
Occasional freelancer Medium — project quality varies Inconsistent Ad hoc Variable, unpredictable

The construction companies with the strongest marketing presence — consistent LinkedIn output, current capability documents, polished tender submissions — all have a design partner who knows their business and owns the production of their marketing materials.

What the right setup looks like in both sectors

Whether you're an agency principal or a construction company director, the pattern is the same: the businesses with the most consistent output have one person — or one studio — who knows their brand inside out and owns the production without needing to be briefed from scratch every time.

Not a rotating cast of freelancers. Not an admin person doing design "when needed." Someone who knows your colours, your tone, your formats, and your deadlines — and delivers to that standard every time.
