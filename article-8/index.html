Graphic Designer for Real Estate Agents in Melbourne — What to Look For

Most graphic designers can make something look good. That's not the problem.

The problem is finding one who understands that you need a listing kit turned around before the weekend, a social tile before the open home, and an agent profile updated because someone just got promoted — all at the same time, every week, without the quality dropping.

If you're an individual agent or a top performer within a larger network, your design needs are different from the agency's. You're self-funding. You're building a personal brand on top of a corporate one. And you need someone who can move at your pace, not theirs.

Handled Studio is a Melbourne design studio that works with real estate agents and agencies across Australia and New Zealand on listing collateral, social assets, and ongoing production.

Here's what to actually look for.

The difference between a graphic designer and a production designer

Most designers are project-based. You brief them, they produce something, you review it, they revise it, and the job is done. That works well for a logo or a one-off brochure.

Real estate marketing doesn't work like that. It's volume, repetition, and speed. The same types of assets — listing kits, social tiles, window cards, EDM headers — produced over and over, every week, to a consistent standard. That's not project work. That's production.

A production designer thinks differently. They learn your brand once and execute quickly. They build templates that make every subsequent job faster. They know your headshot placement, your preferred copy tone, and which printer you use — without being told every time.

When you're evaluating a designer for ongoing real estate work, ask them directly: do you work on retainer or project-by-project? The answer tells you a lot.

What real estate agents actually produce each month

Before you brief anyone, it's worth mapping your real output. Most agents underestimate the volume until they write it down.

Typical monthly design output for a real estate agent

Output type Typical monthly volume Who usually produces it
Listing kits (brochure, flyer, window card) 4–12 per month Agent, PA, or VA
Social tiles (Instagram, Facebook) 12–20 per month Agent or Canva DIY
EDM header / campaign tile 1–2 per month Agency marketing team
Sold / just listed announcements 4–8 per month Agent or admin
Agent profile / bio updates As needed Admin or freelancer
Open home signage Weekly Print supplier

If you're producing 30–50 assets per month — which many top performers are — a project-based designer can't keep up. The turnaround times don't work and the briefing overhead adds up fast.

What to ask before you hire

Do you have experience with real estate collateral specifically?

Real estate has its own visual language — the way listing kits are laid out, how sold strips work, what a compliant property flyer needs to include. A designer who's never worked in the category will spend the first three months learning what you already know.

Can you show me examples of listing kits and agent profiles?

Not just logos or brand identity work. You want to see production output — the everyday stuff, not the award-winning stuff.

What's your turnaround time for a standard listing kit?

If the answer is "3–5 business days," that won't work when a vendor signs on Tuesday and wants the brochure before the open home on Saturday.

How do you handle revisions?

Revision cycles are where freelancer relationships break down. Get clarity on what's included before you start.

Are you available on short notice?

Real estate doesn't run to a production schedule. Understand whether your designer can flex when you need them to.

Where most agents go wrong

Going offshore for cost. Offshore VAs and designers are cheap. They're also not available in your time zone when something urgent lands, don't know what a Section 32 looks like, and produce work that often needs to be rebuilt anyway. The saving rarely holds.

Using Canva without locked templates. Canva is genuinely useful when it's set up properly — with a locked brand kit, approved templates, and fonts that can't be changed. Without that governance, Canva becomes a liability. Every time someone "improves" a template, your brand drifts a little further.

Briefing by example. Sending a designer a photo of another agent's marketing and saying "something like this" is not a brief. It gets you something like something else — not something that's distinctly yours.

Not setting a standard. Without a clear answer to "what does good look like for our brand," every job becomes a negotiation. Set the standard once, in writing, and everything after that is execution.

What the right setup actually looks like

The agents we work with who have the most consistent output all have one thing in common: one person who knows their brand inside out and owns the production every month.

Not a rotating cast of freelancers. Not a VA who does design "when needed." One person — or one studio — who knows your colours, your copy tone, your agents, and your turnaround requirements without being briefed from scratch every time.
